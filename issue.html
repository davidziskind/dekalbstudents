Background & Analysis

The Issue

What is the School Alignment Process, why is DeKalb pursuing it, and why do we believe it's the wrong approach for our students and communities?

Background

What is the School Alignment Process?

The School Alignment Process (SAP) is DeKalb County School District's initiative to evaluate and "right-size" its school portfolio in response to declining enrollment, aging facilities, and budget pressures.

In 2025, DCSD proposed closing or consolidating 27 schools across the county — one of the largest single-cycle school closure proposals in Georgia history. The district argues this is necessary to reduce operational costs and improve resource allocation.

Our Position

Why we oppose this plan

We are not anti-change. We are pro-transparency, pro-community, and pro-children. The SAP as proposed fails on all three counts.

Flawed Criteria

DeKalb's utilization formulas use building capacity numbers that haven't been updated in years. Schools deemed "underutilized" by the district's own metric are often operating at or near practical capacity when you account for current standards for classroom size, specialty space, and ADA compliance.

See the data problems

Self-Inflicted Decline

Many of the "low enrollment" schools are low-enrollment because of district decisions: boundary changes, feeder pattern adjustments, magnet programs that siphon students, and years of deferred maintenance that discourage families from choosing these schools.

Inadequate Community Process

The engagement process gave communities weeks, not months, to respond to proposals affecting decades-old institutions. Input from parents, teachers, and principals was gathered but not demonstrably incorporated into the final recommendations.

Questionable Savings

The district projects significant cost savings — but independent analysis suggests the actual net savings, after accounting for transportation increases, receiving school capacity improvements, and staff redistribution costs, may be far smaller than claimed.

No Clear Educational Case

The district has not presented compelling evidence that consolidation will improve student outcomes. The research on school consolidation at the elementary level is mixed at best — and the disruption costs are well-documented.
